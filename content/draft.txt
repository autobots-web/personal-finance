Title: Last Call Before March 31: The NPS Tax Hack That Can Save You ₹15,000+ Before the Financial Year Ends

Date: 2026-03-27

Content:

**Opening**

With just four days left before the financial year closes on March 31, 2026, millions of Indian salaried taxpayers are racing to squeeze every rupee of tax savings they can. But there is one powerful, government-backed savings tool that most people either overlook entirely or use only halfway — the National Pension System (NPS). If you have not yet made your NPS contribution for FY 2025-26, today could be the most financially productive day of your year.

**What You Need to Know**

- NPS offers a separate tax deduction of ₹50,000 that is not part of the ₹1.5 lakh limit under Section 80C — it sits entirely on top of it.
- Under Section 80CCD(1B), investors can claim this extra ₹50,000 deduction by contributing to their NPS Tier-I account after exhausting the ₹1.5 lakh limit — but only under the old tax regime.
- The most powerful and least-used NPS benefit is under Section 80CCD(2), which covers the employer's contribution to NPS — up to 14% of basic salary plus DA — and this benefit is available under both the old and the new tax regime.
- The ₹50,000 additional NPS deduction under Section 80CCD(1B) remains available for FY 2025-26.
- At maturity, 60% of the NPS corpus can be withdrawn tax-free, while the remaining 40% must be used to buy an annuity, which is then taxed as income.
- The March 31 deadline is hard — contributions made from April 1 onwards count for FY 2026-27, not the current year.

**Why This Matters for Your Wallet**

For a taxpayer in the 30% income tax slab, claiming the additional ₹50,000 deduction under Section 80CCD(1B) directly translates to a tax saving of ₹15,000 plus applicable surcharge and cess. That is money that stays in your pocket simply by routing ₹50,000 into a retirement account before midnight on March 31. For someone in the 20% slab, the saving is ₹10,000. Few investments offer such a direct, guaranteed, government-backed return on your money on day one.

The bigger and less-discussed opportunity is for salaried employees who can negotiate their CTC structure. Tax planners say employees can ask their HR or payroll department to route part of their cost-to-company (CTC) as employer NPS contribution, which can significantly reduce tax without increasing the employer's cost. This restructuring works under both tax regimes and can yield deductions worth lakhs over a career, with zero additional outflow for the employer.

A common mistake is treating NPS purely as a tax-saving instrument and ignoring it for the rest of the year. NPS is also one of the lowest-cost retirement investment products available in India, with fund management charges as low as 0.09% per annum. NPS allows higher deductions than PPF or EPF, which are limited to ₹1.5 lakh under Section 80C, making it a more powerful deduction tool for high earners. Starting early and contributing consistently builds a retirement corpus that compounds significantly over 20-30 years.

**The Details You Need to Know**

- Tax benefits under NPS are available in three parts: Section 80CCD(1) for employee contribution within the ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit, Section 80CCD(1B) for the extra ₹50,000 deduction, and Section 80CCD(2) for employer contributions.
- Under the old tax regime: employee contribution up to ₹1.5 lakh under 80C, an extra ₹50,000 under 80CCD(1B), and employer contribution up to 14% of salary under 80CCD(2).
- Under the new tax regime: no deduction for employee self-contribution, but employer contribution qualifies for deduction up to 14% of salary under Section 80CCD(2) for both government and private sectors.
- Only contributions to NPS Tier-I qualify for these deductions — Tier-II accounts do not offer tax benefits for private or non-government employees.
- Eligible age: Indian citizens and NRIs between 18 and 70 years of age can open an NPS account.
- Recent reforms allow subscribers to withdraw up to 80% of their corpus as a lump sum at retirement, up from the earlier 60%, while at least 20% must still be used to buy an annuity. NPS accounts can now also be used as collateral for loans of up to 25% of personal contributions.

**What Experts Say**

Capital markets banker and investment expert Aditya Garg points out that many investors are unaware that NPS provides a separate tax deduction of ₹50,000 that is entirely outside the ₹1.5 lakh Section 80C limit. According to him, NPS gives you that ₹50,000 in extra deduction not inside your 80C limit, but on top of it. This distinction is critical — most people assume that once they have exhausted their 80C limit through ELSS, PPF, or life insurance premiums, there is nothing more to do. NPS breaks that ceiling.

The Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) has notified the Income-tax Rules, 2026, to align with the new Income Tax Act, which replaces several provisions of the earlier Income-tax Act, 1961 and simplifies compliance by reducing the number of forms and disclosures. Under the new framework, the new tax regime remains the default option. This means that unless you actively opt for the old regime while filing your ITR, you will be placed in the new regime by default — and miss the 80CCD(1B) benefit entirely.

Financial planners across the country are urging their clients to evaluate regime choice carefully before the year-end. If your total deductions including 80C, 80D, and HRA exceed ₹2.5 lakh, sticking to the old tax regime is likely more beneficial. If you have no other deductions or exemptions and your employer contributes to NPS, the new regime may work better for you. A quick calculation with a CA or a free online tax calculator can clarify the right choice in minutes.

**How to Take Action Today**

If you already have an NPS Tier-I account, log in immediately at the NPS Trust portal (npstrust.org.in) or through your bank's net banking interface. Most major banks including SBI, HDFC, ICICI, and Kotak offer seamless NPS contribution through their apps and websites. Contributions made online reflect within 24-48 hours, so March 29 or 30 is your practical last window. Do not wait until March 31 — payment gateway delays on the last day are common.

If you do not yet have an NPS account, you can open one online through eNPS at enps.nsdl.com using your Aadhaar and PAN. The account opening process takes under 30 minutes and the contribution can be made in the same session. Your PRAN (Permanent Retirement Account Number) is generated instantly. Ensure you choose Tier-I (not Tier-II) to be eligible for the tax deduction.

For salaried employees, start a conversation with your HR or payroll team in early April about restructuring your CTC to include employer NPS contributions under Section 80CCD(2). This is a zero-cost restructuring for your employer and can yield significant tax benefits for you every single year going forward, regardless of which tax regime you choose.

**Key Takeaways**

1. The NPS ₹50,000 deduction under Section 80CCD(1B) is available only under the old tax regime and must be claimed before March 31, 2026 — a taxpayer in the 30% slab saves ₹15,000 in tax.
2. Employer NPS contributions under Section 80CCD(2) are deductible under both old and new tax regimes, up to 14% of basic salary plus DA — this is the most powerful and underused NPS benefit.
3. Only NPS Tier-I contributions qualify for tax deductions; Tier-II accounts do not offer this benefit for private sector employees.
4. The new tax regime is the default from FY 2025-26 — if you want to claim the extra ₹50,000 deduction, you must actively opt for the old regime when filing your ITR.
5. NPS now allows up to 80% corpus withdrawal as a lump sum at retirement, and accounts can be pledged as loan collateral for up to 25% of your own contributions — making it more flexible than before.
6. Salaried employees should request CTC restructuring to include employer NPS contributions from April 1, 2026, to maximise tax savings throughout FY 2026-27.
7. The deadline is absolute — contributions made on or after April 1 count for the next financial year; act before March 31 to secure this year's deduction.

**What to Watch Next**

The new law restricts adjustment of certain losses while computing total income, and loss under the head house property cannot be adjusted against income from other heads if the taxpayer opts for the concessional regime. This makes tax planning for FY 2026-27 more nuanced than ever, especially for those with home loans or investment deductions. Taxpayers should consult a CA in April to evaluate their regime choice for the new financial year.

Watch for the RBI's next Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) meeting. Rate decisions in 2026 will influence fixed deposit returns, debt mutual fund yields, and the attractiveness of NPS equity versus hybrid allocations. India's banking system is currently benefiting from strong structural drivers, with retail credit demand remaining strong particularly in housing and personal loans, and the overall regulatory tone remaining balanced — supportive of growth but cautious about inflation. Any rate cut could make the equity-heavy NPS option more attractive relative to FDs.

Looking further ahead, PFRDA is expected to introduce more flexible NPS withdrawal norms and potentially expand the NPS Vatsalya scheme for minors. Keeping an eye on PFRDA circulars and the Union Budget announcements mid-year will help you fine-tune your retirement contribution strategy for the rest of FY 2026-27.

**The Bottom Line**

With just four days left in the financial year, contributing ₹50,000 to your NPS Tier-I account is one of the simplest, highest-return financial decisions you can make today — saving up to ₹15,600 in taxes while building your retirement corpus. Log in, contribute, and lock in your savings before March 31.
